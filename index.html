01 / The Thesis

What I read.
Before building this.

The thesis page defines the mandate as "unlocking value from direct access to a large install base of young, empowered, digitally native and highly selective consumers." The Consumption Report 2025 maps this across three upgrade frontiers (Bharat Core to Value Upgraders, Value Upgraders to Urban Aspirants, Urban Aspirants to Affluent India), four income-based archetypes, and six aspirational archetypes: The Self-Optimizer, The Silver Economy, The Modern Parent, The Indie Luxe Shopper, The Tier 2/3 Aspirant, and The Household CFO.

The portfolio map and gap analysis below are built against that framework.

02.5 / Archetype Fit

Three gaps, mapped to
the archetypes they serve.

Each of the three categories below maps directly to the archetypes that already define the portfolio's consumer conviction.

Gap 01
Pet Care
Primary archetypes
Urban Aspirants Affluent India
The first-time pet parent in India is almost entirely the Urban Aspirant: dual income, urban, treats pets as family. Combined growth of Urban Aspirants and Affluent India is +96M households by 2034.
Aspirational archetype fit
The Modern Parent (conscious choices, baby care, kids nutrition) sits within the Urban Aspirants and Affluent India income bands, the same two archetypes primary to pet care. Conscious choices for children extend naturally to pets in the same household. The Self-Optimizer (health, performance, active care) is the Gen Z and millennial Urban Aspirant who applies the same health-first lens to their pets as to themselves.
Gap 02
Athleisure
Primary archetypes
Urban Aspirants Affluent India
Urban Aspirants (83M HH growing to 122M by 2034) are the core buyer: identity-led, fitness-conscious, brand-first. The consumption report lists fashion and tech as their primary categories. Activewear sits at the intersection of both. 110M Indians actively participated in fitness in 2024, and every one of them buys clothing for it.
Aspirational archetype fit
The Self-Optimizer (health, performance, personal best) is the primary. Activewear is the most visible expression of a fitness identity, worn beyond the gym into daily life. The Indie Luxe Shopper (identity, design, status) is the secondary: choosing an Indian brand over Nike as a deliberate identity statement about supporting domestic design.
Gap 03
Fragrance
Primary archetypes
Urban Aspirants Affluent India
The 3one4 Consumption Report lists beauty and fashion as Urban Aspirant category preferences. Fragrance sits at the intersection of both. Affluent India (+57M HH by 2034) is the secondary buyer for niche and premium positioning.
Aspirational archetype fit
The Indie Luxe Shopper (identity, design, status, premium fashion and beauty) is the primary. Fragrance is one of the clearest expressions of identity-led purchasing: you choose a scent to signal who you are. The Self-Optimizer (wellness, active care) maps to the wellness-adjacent fragrance sub-category, aromatherapy and mood-linked scents, which brands like Secret Alchemist are already building.

Why this fits the pattern

Buying pet food in India today mostly means unorganised markets, inconsistent quality, no nutritional labelling, and zero veterinary guidance. 70% of current Indian pet parents are first-time owners. For most of them, the dominant behaviour is anxiety, not habit. A brand that solves trust, quality, and convenience for this buyer has a long runway before it faces a credible challenger.

Buying pet food from an unorganised market, the default for most Indian pet owners, means inconsistent quality, no nutritional labelling, and zero veterinary guidance. The first-time pet parent (70% of current owners) is anxious, aspirational, and willing to pay for trust. They want what the urban Indian meat buyer wanted in 2016: a brand they can rely on.

The FMCG pile-in is the clearest signal that the category has crossed a threshold. Reliance launched Waggies, Wipro launched HappyFur, Godrej launched Ninja, all in 2025-26. Nestlé Purina has called India a "mega-market." Incumbents validate categories; founders own them. The sequence is now in motion.

The structural gap

Over 110 million Indians actively participated in fitness, gym workouts, running, and sports in 2024 (Maximize Market Research). Every one of them buys clothing for it. E-commerce sportswear sales grew 32% in 2024, with Myntra, Amazon, and Flipkart becoming primary discovery channels (IMARC).

The market today is Decathlon at the value end and Nike, Adidas, and Puma at premium — with the top five global players accounting for 55-60% of branded sportswear revenue in India (IMARC). The middle is almost entirely empty. An Indian, identity-led, premium-but-accessible activewear brand that understands Indian gym culture, Indian body types, and Indian climate does not exist at scale. BlissClub proved this works for women. The men's equivalent — tank tops, joggers, printed gym wear built around Indian gym culture — has no dominant player. Men's athleisure is now the fastest-growing segment in the category (Market Research Future).

Why this fits 3one4's pattern

The consumption report identifies Urban Aspirants (identity and lifestyle) and the Self-Optimizer (health, performance, personal best) as two of the six aspirational archetypes driving the next decade of consumer spending. Activewear sits at the exact intersection of both. It is purchased for performance and worn as identity.

Why this fits the pattern

Fragrance in India today has a clear structural gap at the mid-market. The traditional category is dominated by attars (fragmented, unorganised) at the low end and imported luxury brands at the premium end. There is almost nothing in the middle, an Indian, identity-led, premium-but-accessible fragrance brand that understands Indian climate, Indian skin chemistry, and India's cultural relationship with scent.

The India-specific angle is the moat. Attar culture, covering gifting, religious use, and heritage, is a brand identity that imported luxury brands cannot access. A founder building around that cultural context owns terrain that no foreign brand can replicate.
